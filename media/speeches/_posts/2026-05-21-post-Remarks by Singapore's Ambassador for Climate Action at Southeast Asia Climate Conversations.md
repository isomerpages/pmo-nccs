<p><strong>KEYNOTE REMARKS BY SINGAPORE'S AMBASSADOR FOR CLIMATE ACTION&nbsp;AT SOUTHEAST ASIA CLIMATE CONVERSATIONS, MADAME FAN, SINGAPORE, 21 MAY 2026</strong>
</p>
<hr>
<h4><strong>A Climate Agenda for Southeast Asia</strong></h4>
<p>Distinguished guests, friends and colleagues, good evening.</p>
<p>Let me start with two uncomfortable truths.</p>
<p><em>Southeast Asia accounts for 6% of global emissions and they are continuing to rise. Southeast Asia is the most climate‑vulnerable region in the world, along with South Asia. </em>In
short, we have a serious mitigation problem and a serious adaptation problem.</p>
<p>THE STATE OF MITIGATION IN SOUTHEAST ASIA</p>
<p>Let me start with our mitigation problem.</p>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia is the only major region in the world where growth and emissions have not decoupled.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The major advanced economies are continuing to grow while reducing emissions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In Asia, China's emissions have peaked and are now declining while the
country continues to grow.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In India, emissions won't peak until the 2040s but its emissions are growing
slower than its economy.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In Southeast Asia, unless we decouple growth in GDP and growth in emissions,
we will be forced to choose one over the other. &nbsp;That’s not a choice
we want to make.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>There are <strong><em>three forces driving emissions in Southeast Asia</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>First, <strong><em>deforestation and land-use change</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Agriculture, forestry, and land use account for more than half of Southeast
Asia's emissions. <sup>[1]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ASEAN contributes around 30% of global agriculture and land-use emissions. <sup>[2]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Expansion of agriculture, logging, and plantations drives ongoing forest
loss.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Second,<strong><em> the power sector still runs on fossil fuels.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Fossil fuels account for three-quarters of ASEAN's electricity generation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Coal alone contributes 44%. <sup>[3]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Third,<strong><em> rapid economic growth is driving industrial emissions.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>As economies industrialise, emissions from manufacturing, cement, steel,
and transport are rising.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>THE STATE OF ADAPTATION IN SOUTHEAST ASIA</p>
<p>Next, our adaptation problem.</p>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia is the most climate‑vulnerable region in the world, along with South Asia.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Asia as a whole is warming nearly twice as fast as the global average.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Some parts of Southeast Asia may experience 60 more days of extreme heat
annually by mid-century.<sup>[4]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sea level rise around Southeast Asia exceeds the global mean, threatening
more than 150 million people living in low-lying coastal areas. <sup>[5]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The toll on lives and livelihoods is mounting:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Southeast Asia faces approximately 100 climate disasters annually, affecting
80 million people each year.<sup>[6]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>And these costs are not evenly distributed. They fall disproportionately
on:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>vulnerable communities,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>smallholder farmers, and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>low-income households.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>STRATEGIES TO RESOLVE SOUTHEAST ASIA’S TRILEMMA</p>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia is facing a trilemma.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>First, <strong><em>we must grow</em></strong>. Our 680 million people deserve
rising living standards and economic opportunity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Second, <strong><em>we must decarbonise</em></strong>. Our energy demand
will double by 2040<sup>[7]</sup> and how we meet it matters globally, not
just regionally.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Third, <strong><em>we must adapt</em></strong>. Climate change has already
begun in our region and will only get worse: rising seas, extreme heat,
devastating floods.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Resolving this trilemma requires governments, industry, and finance to
work in concert. And that’s why we are gathered here.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>As we prepare for our ASEAN chairmanship next year, Singapore is looking at three areas where we can work together to address the trilemma.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Carbon markets – to mobilise capital for decarbonisation</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nature solutions – to tackle Southeast Asia's biggest source of emissions</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Climate resilience – to protect lives and livelihoods amidst climate change</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>CARBON MARKETS</p>
<p>Let me start with carbon markets.</p>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia has huge potential for decarbonisation projects that can generate high-quality carbon credits.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Mangrove restoration, peatland protection, and avoided deforestation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Methane capture from palm oil mills.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The phase-out of coal through an emerging class of energy transition credits.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><em>But carbon markets face two challenges that limit their effectiveness.</em>
</p>
<p><strong><em>First, an integrity challenge.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Some carbon projects in the past have overstated their impact:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>claiming reductions that would have happened anyway</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>failing to deliver promised results.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Some buyers exploited this, purchasing cheap, low-quality credits to avoid
genuine decarbonisation instead of reducing their own emissions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>This has eroded trust in the voluntary carbon markets.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Second, a financing challenge.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Many good decarbonisation projects struggle to secure financing:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>high upfront costs</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>uncertain revenue streams</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>long payback periods.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>The answer is not to abandon carbon markets, but to rebuild integrity so that financing can follow.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We must continue to work on robust verification of emissions reduction,
conservative baselines, and real additionality testing.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Singapore is working with partners to strengthen the supply side, demand side, and infrastructure underpinnings of carbon markets</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We are collaborating with stakeholders such as the Integrity Council for
the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) to improve supply-side integrity in
the carbon markets.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We are investing in technology solutions for better MRV (monitoring, reporting,
verification).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We are signalling genuine demand by procuring high-integrity credits through
Article 6 agreements that count towards our national climate targets.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We are providing clarity to corporate buyers on the responsible use of
carbon credits through the international Coalition to Grow Carbon Markets,
which Singapore co-chairs with Kenya and the United Kingdom.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><em>We look forward to stepping up collaboration with our ASEAN neighbours to build a vibrant carbon market ecosystem in the region.</em>
</p>
<p>NATURE SOLUTIONS</p>
<p>Next, nature-based solutions.</p>
<p><strong><em>For Southeast Asia, nature offers the greatest mitigation potential, while also strengthening resilience to climate impacts.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Mangroves protect coastlines from storm surges and erosion.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Peatlands store carbon and stabilise local climates.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Forests regulate rainfall and stabilise water supplies</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sustainable agriculture improves crop yields, farmer incomes, and resilience
to heat and drought.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia holds vast untapped nature-based carbon removal potential.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We have about a quarter of the world's forest carbon capacity, over a
third of the world’s mangroves, and nearly all its tropical peatlands.<sup>[8]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>But nature is under-invested in Southeast Asia.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The region needs US$54 billion annually by 2030 for nature-based solutions
but is getting only US$8 billion.<sup>[9]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>There are<strong><em> three reasons why nature is under-invested.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>First,<strong><em> most of nature’s value is not priced</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>the carbon sequestered by a mangrove forest,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>the flood protection it provides, or</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>the nursery habitat it offers to fisheries</p>
<p>– none of these appear on any balance sheet</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Second,<strong><em> nature has a measurement challenge.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The outcomes of nature projects are complex, variable, and difficult to
quantify.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>How much carbon does a particular mangrove stand sequester?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>What happens to emissions if the restored forest is disturbed ten years
from now.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Third,<strong><em> most nature projects are not bankable.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Their governance is complex, involving multiple stakeholders.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Their revenue models are thin, their returns are long-duration.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>And their transaction costs are high relative to project size.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>The solutions lies in environmental integrity, innovative financing, and demand mobilisation.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Environmental integrity through better measurements.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Investment in remote sensing, ground data, and models built for Southeast
Asia's ecosystems.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The more certain we are about the carbon numbers, the lower the risk premium&nbsp;
and the more capital will flow.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Innovations like blended finance coupled with carbon credits.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use public and philanthropic capital to de-risk projects to catalyse multiples
of commercial capital.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Singapore is already using blended finance to de-risk renewable energy
and sustainable transport projects across Asia. Could the same structure
potentially work for mangrove restoration and peatland protection?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Demand mobilisation through buyer coalitions and offtake agreements.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>To developers confidence: if you build a credible project, someone will
pay for it.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><em>What does success look like in the nature space?</em>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p><em>When a mangrove restoration project in Southeast Asia is as financeable as a new industrial park.</em>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><em>That is what ASEAN needs to work together on.</em>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>CLIMATE RESILIENCE</p>
<p>Last but not least, climate resilience.</p>
<p><strong><em>Relative to its needs, Asia is the most underserved by adaptation finance.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Estimates suggest that the Asia-Pacific requires up to US$400 billion
annually for climate adaptation.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Actual flows in 2021-22 were US$34 billion; that is less than one dollar
flowing for every twelve dollars needed.<sup>[10]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Adaptation finance is held back by three limitations.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>First, <strong><em>the returns are diffused</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>A sea wall protects the factory, the fishing village, the public road,
and the neighbourhood behind it.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The investor who built it cannot charge most of those beneficiaries.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Second,<strong><em> the returns are public goods.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Early warning systems, flood maps, drainage upgrades, heat action plans
– once provided, everyone benefits whether they paid or not.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Third,<strong><em> the returns are difficult to count</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Adaptation pays off as disasters that do not happen. And what does not
happen never appears on a balance sheet.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>There is no revenue line for a flood that was avoided.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>There is no return‑on‑investment calculation for a heat wave that did
not kill workers.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Adaptation is a system problem. We cannot solve it in silos. We need a systemic response.&nbsp;</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Singapore has been consulting with partners across the ecosystem—governments,
corporates, financial institutions—on designing a holistic, systemic approach.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A systemic response comprises five interconnected priorities.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>First, good national adaptation plans.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>A good national adaptation plan addresses three things:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p><strong>identifies the climate risks,</strong>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>specifies the investments needed, and</strong>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>assigns responsibilities – what government will fund and deliver, what the private sector can be incentivised to do, and where public‑private partnership is needed.</strong>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Some ASEAN countries like Thailand, the Philippines, and Vietnam have
already submitted plans. &nbsp;Singapore is preparing its first national
adaptation plan.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>If governments can credibly present adaptation as an investment proposition, they can tap on capital markets.</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>A notable example is the Tokyo Resilience Bond — the first bond certified
under the Climate Bonds Standard. At EUR 300 million, it was oversubscribed
seven times.<sup>[11]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Second, corporate transition plans that integrate climate resilience.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Many companies have started to assess physical climate risks at key facilities,
but most still miss the transboundary effects that can affect business
continuity across their supply chains.<sup>[12]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Companies need to map climate risks across their:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>upstream suppliers</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>logistics corridors and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>the infrastructure their operations depend on.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Once risks are mapped, companies must decide how to respond:</p>
<p>build resilience into operations</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; diversify suppliers or.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>secure insurance</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Third, price physical climate risk into financial decisions.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p><strong>Financial firms must integrate physical climate risk into their investment and lending decisions.</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>A loan to a coastal developer in a flood‑prone region should reflect the
asset’s climate exposure over its lifetime.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Financial institutions that do not price physical climate risk into their
portfolios are carrying hidden liabilities.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Fourth, blended finance structures to catalyse private capital.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>As with marginally bankable decarbonisation projects, blended finance
that synergises public, private, and philanthropic capital is the way to
go for adaptation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Governments can take the lead in setting up such vehicles to improve the
bankability of adaptation projects and crowd-in private capital.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Fifth—and foundational to all the above—make adaptation measurable.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In mitigation, we have a universal unit – tonnes of carbon dioxide reduced
or removed – so investments, targets, and accountability can all be framed
around this.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Adaptation has no equivalent.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>A sea wall built, a drainage system upgraded, a heat action plan implemented
– we have no shared metric to aggregate or compare these efforts across
projects and sectors.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Governments, corporates, and financial institutions must collectively
invest in climate risk data and analytics to make adaptation measurable.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>CLOSING</p>
<p>Let me conclude.</p>
<p>The issues before us for discussion: carbon markets, nature solutions,
and climate resilience are not abstractions.&nbsp;</p>
<p>They are real for hundreds of millions of our people:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Farmers in the Mekong Delta abandoning rice fields as saltwater advances
inland.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Jakarta's urban poor lose days of work with every flood.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Coastal communities in the Philippines rebuilding homes with their own
savings, one typhoon at a time</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>They are adapting, but without the finance and technology to do it faster,
cheaper, and at scale.</p>
<p>Today, as we discuss, let us focus on concrete moves.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>What needs to change in policy, in capital deployment, in your own institutions?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>To make carbon markets, nature solutions, and climate resilience work
at scale.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>If we get this right, we strengthen our region's competitiveness and safeguard
the lives and livelihoods of hundreds of millions of our people.</p>
