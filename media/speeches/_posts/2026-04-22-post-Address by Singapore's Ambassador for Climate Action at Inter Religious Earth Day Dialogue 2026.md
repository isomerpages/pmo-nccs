<p><strong>ADDRESS BY SINGAPORE'S AMBASSADOR FOR CLIMATE ACTION AT INTER RELIGIOUS EARTH DAY DIALOGUE 2026</strong>
</p>
<hr>
<h4><strong>Responsibility, Reverence, Restraint</strong></h4>
<p>Honourable leaders of faith, ladies and gentlemen, good afternoon.</p>
<p>Thank you Caritas Singapore for bringing together, on this day dedicated
to our planet, voices of faith from so many traditions.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>THE SILENT KILLERS</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The world is transfixed by the daily news cycle: devastating wars, energy
crisis, trade frictions, and rising cost of living.</p>
<p>But there are two <em>silent killers</em> which are slowly but surely affecting
lives and livelihoods.</p>
<p>And, if unchecked, will become existential threats affecting all of humanity:
climate change and nature loss.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>Climate change is no longer a distant threat — it is our daily reality.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Global temperatures are continuing to increase, sea levels are rising
faster than in the last 3000 years, the polar ice caps are melting faster
than expected, and glaciers are retreating at record pace.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We saw its impact in the catastrophic floods in Pakistan in 2022 that
affected over 30 million people and submerged a third of the country.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We saw it again last year: the third-hottest year ever recorded. Heat
stress now kills one person every minute globally.<sup>[i]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The climate crisis we ignore today will be the defining crisis of tomorrow.<strong>&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Nature loss is accelerating — with dire consequences for water and food security.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We see it in the rapid deforestation of the Amazon and Southeast Asia,
where critical ecosystems that regulate climate and water cycles are being
degraded.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nearly half the world’s population lives with severe water scarcity for
at least one month a year.<sup>[ii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We see it in the collapse of biodiversity—nearly one million species at
risk of extinction, many within decades. In Southeast Asia, up to 42% of
all species could be lost.<sup>[iii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We see it in the decline of insect pollinators, which threatens global
food production, and in the degradation of soils that underpin agriculture.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>THE TWIN TRAGEDIES</strong>
</p>
<p>Climate change and nature loss are not just scientific or economic problems.
They are moral problems, driven by two well-known dynamics: the <em>tragedy of the horizons</em> and
the <em>tragedy of the commons</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>The tragedy of the horizons is that we vastly prioritise the present over the future.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The decisions that will shape our climate future are made in the present.
But the costs of climate change fall largely on the future.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>And so we delay. We focus on short-term gains over long-term survival.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ultimately, this means much larger long-term costs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>By delaying the action and investments needed today, we are passing an
enormous burden to future generations.<strong>&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>The tragedy of the commons is that we exploit shared resources hoping that others will restrain themselves</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Our planet and our atmosphere are our shared resources. But no one owns
them.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>So, each country, company, or person has an incentive to take more – to
emit more greenhouse gases, to consume more of the planet’s resources.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>But when everyone does the same, it leads to collective ruin in the form
of catastrophic climate change and nature degradation.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>How can we overcome the twin tragedies? This is where faith traditions
come in. They speak to the inner dimensions of these tragedies.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>RESPONSIBILITY, REVERANCE, RESTRAINT</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Faith traditions offer three principles supporting care for the environment:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p><strong><em>responsibility</em></strong><em>: the stewardship of creation</em>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong><em>reverence</em></strong><em>: the interconnectedness of all life</em>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong><em>restraint</em></strong><em>: moderation and simplicity in living</em>&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>First, stewardship of creation </em></strong>—<strong><em> that the Earth is not ours to own but is ours to care for and pass on to future generations.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Judaism tells us: “<em>The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it.”</em><sup>[iv]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Christianity emphasises the principle of stewardship.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In his encyclical <em>Laudato si’</em>, Pope Francis reminds us that the
earth has been given to us to protect, not exploit. This encyclical influenced
the moral framing leading up to the Paris Agreement in 2015, which set
limits on global warming.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Islam speaks of humanity as <em>khalifah</em>—a trustee.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>As the Prophet Muhammad puts it: “<em>If the final hour comes while you have a sapling in your hand, plant it.”</em><sup>[v]</sup><em> </em>Even
at the end of time— act for the future.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Baha’i faith presents nature as a divine trust and a reflection of
God’s majesty.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>A modern saying beautifully summarises this principle of stewardship:</p>
<p><em>We don’t inherit the earth from our ancestors, we borrow it from our children.</em><strong>&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
<p><strong><em>Second, the interconnectedness of all life </em></strong>—<strong><em> we are not separate from nature, we are part of it.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Hinduism teaches <em>“All that exists in this universe is pervaded by the Divine." </em><sup>[vi]</sup>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In this vision, rivers, forests, animals, and the elements are not resources
to be consumed carelessly, but expressions of a deeper divine order. Protecting
nature is not separate from spiritual life.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Zoroastrianism endorses the caring of “seven creations” – sky, water,
earth, plant, animal, human and fire – as part of a symbiotic relationship.<sup>[vii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In Buddhism, interconnectedness is foundational.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The Dalai Lama speaks of the profound interdependence of all phenomena
and reminds us that the suffering of the Earth is inseparable from the
suffering of beings.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Jainism urges respect for all forms of life and protection of the natural
world as part of a moral and religious obligation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Islamic concept of <em>tawhid</em> emphasises the unity of God and creation;
nature is a sign of God, not a mere resource.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Or as Pope Leo puts it: <em>“The universe reflects the face of Jesus Christ, in whom all things were created and redeemed”</em>.<sup>[viii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The founder of Sikhism, Guru Nanak, teaches: <em>“Air is the Guru, Water is the Father, and Earth is the Great Mother of all.”</em><sup>[ix]</sup><em> </em>This
is not metaphor. It is relationship. It is kinship.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>In short, we are part of a larger order; to act against it is to act against
ourselves.</p>
<p><em>Caring for creation is part of loving God and neighbour.</em>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>Third, moderation and simplicity in living. Excessive consumption and waste are key drivers of climate change and nature loss.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In Buddhism, desire is the root of suffering. Contentment comes from appreciating
what we have, not accumulating endless possessions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Jain principle of <em>“aparigraha”</em> – or non-possessiveness – supports
moderation in consumption, low-waste living, and sustainable diets.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Taoism teaches: <em>“He who knows that enough is enough will always have enough."</em><sup>[x]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Christianity calls us to live with gratitude and restraint, pointing out
that <em>“godliness with contentment is great gain.”</em><sup>[xi]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Effective climate action is not just about finance or technology. It is ultimately about individual choices – how we live and what we support</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>It boils down to simple acts of conservation in daily life.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is about supporting policies&nbsp;that protect our environment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is about willingness to bear some inconvenience or cost for a larger
good.<em>&nbsp;</em>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><em>Religious organisations can be a powerful force to shape individual choices.</em>
</p>
<p><em>By emphasising the spiritual principles of stewardship, interconnectedness, and moderation.</em>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>LEADING CLIMATE-FRIENDLY AND NATURE-POSITIVE INITIATIVES</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>Besides shaping individual choices, faith-based organisations can support climate and nature through their direct actions.</em></strong>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>First, incorporating sustainable practices in how you manage your physical assets.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The Lutheran Church of Sweden owns 22% of Sweden's commercial forests.
It has committed to protect 20% of forest areas.<sup>[xii] [xiii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Diocese of Leeds supported 245 churches to switch to renewable electricity
and better manage their energy use.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We see this locally too, in how places of worship are being made greener.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Masjid Al-Aleem Siddique eliminated single-use plastic bottles during
Ramadan and adopted solar energy.<sup>[xiv]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Efforts like these will inspire your congregations and communities, which
include business leaders, professionals, and community influencers.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When you model sustainable practices, they bring these values into their
professional decisions, creating ripple effects beyond the immediate community.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Second, supporting climate and nature through philanthropy and community action.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Islamic endowments are adopting new <em>"green waqf"</em> frameworks to
finance renewable energy and climate projects.<sup>[xv]</sup>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In Indonesia, a "Sharing Electricity" waqf project funds solar systems
for off-grid villages.<sup>[xvi]</sup>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In 2022, WaterAid raised US$250,000 to provide clean water in climate-affected
communities.<sup>[xvii]</sup>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Buddhist Tzu Chi Foundation, active in over 95 countries, advocates
community recycling, vegetarian diets, and resource conservation.<a href="#_edn18" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xviii]</a>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>In Malaysia, they diverted over half a million tonnes of waste from landfills
since 1995.<a href="#_edn19" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xix]</a>
</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In Taiwan, they recycled over 1.4 million tonnes of paper, saving over
28 million trees over the past 20 years.<a href="#_edn20" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xx]</a>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Mata Amritanandamayi Math from India has framed climate action as
a “<em>revolution of compassion”</em>.<a href="#_edn21" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xxi]</a>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>It has planted more than one million trees globally, cleaned up rivers,
promoted composting and recycling, and launched organic food cultivation
programmes – to reduce carbon emissions and strengthen local resilience.
<a href="#_edn22" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xxii]</a>&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Third, helping communities adapt and thrive in a carbon-constrained, climate-impaired world.</em></strong>
</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We are heading into a world where communities have to deal with two challenges
at the same time:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Mitigation – restructuring our economies and adjusting our lifestyles
as the world is forced to step up decarbonisation amidst worsening climate
change.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Adaptation – building up resilience against the consequences of climate
change on how we live and how we work.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia's transition to a low-carbon future will entail significant socio-economic trade-offs</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Many regional countries still rely on fossil fuels for energy and jobs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>We must ensure that the energy transition is also a just transition.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Faith-based organisations can walk alongside communities, providing support
and reskilling workers.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong><em>Southeast Asia is expected to be most affected by climate change – through heat stress, floods and droughts, and sea-level rise</em></strong>.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The human cost of climate change will be greatest in the poorest and most
fragile places.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Faith-based organisations can play a critical role in helping to build
community resilience to withstand what is coming.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>This could mean supporting farmers to grow drought-resilient crops, helping
build flood-safe housing, and assisting relocation from high-risk areas.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Closer to home, it could mean helping lower-income households protect
themselves against heat stress as temperatures continue to climb.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>Religious organisations will also have a growing role in providing relief
amidst worsening natural disasters. They are already stepping up.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>When typhoons slammed into the Philippines in recent years, churches and
faith‑based agencies from across the region mobilised immediately, not
only providing shelter and necessities but also helping to rebuild homes
and livelihoods.<a href="#_edn23" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xxiii]</a> 
<a href="#_edn24" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">[xxiv]</a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><em>Climate change does not respect national boundaries or differentiate religions. To deal with it, we must collaborate across faiths and across borders.</em><strong>&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
<p><strong>A SHARED MORAL CALLING&nbsp;</strong>
</p>
<p>We began with the two tragedies underpinning climate change:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>the tragedy of the horizons and the tragedy of the commons.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Both arise from a narrow view of self: a self concerned</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>only with the present, only with its own gain.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Faith traditions expand our view of the self.</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>We are part of something larger. We are accountable to something higher.&nbsp;</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Climate action is thus not only policy. It is not only economics. It is
a moral and spiritual calling.&nbsp;</p>
<p>We come from different faith traditions, walk different paths. But we
share some abiding spiritual truths:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>The Earth is a trust.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Life is interconnected.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Moderation is virtue.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The common language of all religions is <em>love</em> – to care for one
another, to cherish our common humanity, to safeguard our common home.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Let our attitude to our planet be one of responsibility, reverence, and
restraint.</p>
<p>Let us pray to be good stewards and wise custodians, choosing compassion
over indifference, action over inertia, and hope over despair.</p>
